LG UltraFine 5k on Linux and Windows

May 24, 2020

This is yet another post about attempting to get the LG UltraFine 5k working on Linux/Windows.

TLDR: It works at a 4k resolution on my desktop and 5k from my Macbook Pro.

Why are you doing this?

The LG UltraFine 5k is a really gorgeous monitor that’s (unfortunately) a unique product in the market right now.
There seems to be no other product with the following feature sets:

  • A glossy panel
  • 5k resolution
  • Good color reproduction
  • High DPI
  • A single cable docking solution through Thunderbolt
  • Doesn’t have a LOUD or GAMER design
  • Camera, speakers, and mic built in

The most attractive of above listed features is the ability to use the Monitor as a docking solution, where I can use a single cable and have all my peripherals be detected alongside it. With that switching between my work laptop and my personal computer would be a breeze!

I’m primarily a Linux user for my personal setup and I’ve been eyeing this monitor enviously for years. Due to the lack of good Thunderbolt support from OEMs and the special dual Display Port connection over Thunderbolt implementation the older revision of the monitor had I’ve previously been holding off on the purchase.

I decided to take the plunge on the monitor due to the fact that more OEMs have started producing motherboards with Thunderbolt and the newer revision of the monitor now accepts a non-Thunderbolt USB-C connection, albeit at a lower 4k resolution.

My setup

Work

My work setup is a 15” Macbook Pro (2019).
Everything just works as advertised in full glorious 5k resolution where text is super clear.

Home

Alongside the new monitor, I decided to splurge a little on myself and build a new computer. My current setup being:

  • Motherboard: Asrock X570 Phantom Gaming-ITX/TB3 (BIOS: 2.0)
  • CPU: AMD Ryzen 7 3700x
  • GPU: Nvidia 2070 Super Founders Edition
  • Case: NZXT H1
  • RAM: 32GB

It’s a nice little ITX build that was super simple to put together. Other than the glass front panel, I have no complaints!

Though the motherboard has a Titan Ridge Thunderbolt controller, it doesn’t support a dual Display Port input. So I’m limited to a 4k resolution on my home computer. This was a trade off I was willing to accept, hopefully in the future I can find another ITX motherboard that can supports a dual Display Port input.

Windows 10

I’ve been suckered by some friends to once again start using Windows on my personal machine to play some online games with them. I haven’t touched Windows in years and was actually pleasantly surprised.
Everything worked out of the box after installing the drivers provided by the motherboard OEM. Windows was able to detect the speaker, mic, and additional peripherals on my monitor over Thunderbolt just fine.
The recommended display scaling to 1.5 looks just fine to my eyes, and realistically I probably wouldn’t be able to play any games at decent performance at 5k anyhow.

Linux

When I work at home for personal projects I tend to Linux as my primary OS for development. Currently I’m running the following:

  • Distro: Fedora 32
  • Kernel Version: 5.6.13-300
  • DE: GNOME 3.36.2

This is where things started to get hairy. I followed the instructions from here to have GNOME and GDM detect my monitor and the resolution properly by overriding my X11 and GNOME configuration.

But I noticed that all my USB peripherals (including the webcam, mic, and speakers built in the display) were not working. Various log outputs were not showing anything useful and attempts to force power on the usb devices didn’t seems to address the issue. At this point I scoured the web for others with similar issues but to no avail. Documentation on people attempting to use this monitor on non-Macs is pretty sparse.

I almost gave up here and was considering returning this monitor but then I realized that my graphics card actually has a USB-C port which is meant for VR headsets. Figured I had nothing to lose and connected my monitor to it and….everything worked just like it did in Windows!

Cool, so I know I can use the USB-C port on my GPU like a normal USB-C port. But i really wanted to take advantage of the Thunderbolt port on my motherboard, hell it was the whole reason why I bought it!

Hmm upon digging I found this blog post.

TLDR: the USB 3.0 spec is borked. To get around this we want to disable USB-3.0 and force USB-2.0.

So to do that I did the following the instructions here:

lspci -nn | grep USB                                   

# 09:00.0 USB controller [0c03]: Intel Corporation JHL7540 Thunderbolt 3 USB Controller [Titan Ridge DD 2018] [8086:15f0] (rev 06)
# 0a:00.0 USB controller [0c03]: Intel Corporation JHL7540 Thunderbolt 3 USB Controller [Titan Ridge 2C 2018] [8086:15e9] (rev 06)
# 27:00.1 USB controller [0c03]: Advanced Micro Devices, Inc. [AMD] Matisse USB 3.0 Host Controller [1022:149c]
# 27:00.3 USB controller [0c03]: Advanced Micro Devices, Inc. [AMD] Matisse USB 3.0 Host Controller [1022:149c]
# 2a:00.2 USB controller [0c03]: NVIDIA Corporation TU104 USB 3.1 Host Controller [10de:1ad8] (rev a1)
# 2a:00.3 Serial bus controller [0c80]: NVIDIA Corporation TU104 USB Type-C UCSI Controller [10de:1ad9] (rev a1)
# 2c:00.3 USB controller [0c03]: Advanced Micro Devices, Inc. [AMD] Matisse USB 3.0 Host Controller [1022:149c]

setpci -H1 -d 8086:15f d0.1=0
setpci -H1 -d 8086:15e9 d0.1=0
sudo reboot

And….it works just like in Windows!
